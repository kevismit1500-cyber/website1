How Often Do You Test Your Cybersecurity Incident Response Procedures?

1

Preparation

This step focuses on creating policies, training, and tools before an incident occurs. Make sure your organization has a clear security policy, understand your risks, and know which systems and data are most important. Decide what types of incidents matter most and assemble a Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) to handle them.
2

Detection

This step focuses on spotting early warning signs that something may be wrong. Keep an eye on your systems for anything unusual. When something suspicious happens, confirm whether it’s a real security incident. Wireshark is a great security tool for sniffing network traffic. Gather evidence (unusual packets), figure out what type of incident it is, how serious it is, and document everything you find.
3

Analysis

This step focuses on digging into the incident so you can understand exactly what happened and how serious it is. Review the evidence you’ve collected, figure out how the attacker got in, identify which systems or data were affected, and determine the overall impact. Your goal here is to get a clear picture of the incident so you know what to fix first and how to guide the rest of the response.
4

Containment

This step focuses on stopping the incident from spreading and limiting the damage as quickly as possible. Start by taking immediate action, such as isolating affected devices or network segments, to prevent the attacker from moving further. After that, apply longer‑term containment measures. This means temporary fixes that keep your systems running safely while you work on rebuilding clean, secure versions of the affected systems.
5

Eradication

This step focuses on completely removing the threat and fixing the weaknesses (security patching) that allowed it to happen. Eliminate any malware, malicious files, or unauthorized access from every affected system as soon as possible. Then you track down the root cause — how the attacker got in — and close that gap so the same method can’t be used again. Your goal here is to make sure the environment is clean, stable, and protected before moving on to the recovery phase.
6

Recovery

This step focuses on safely bringing your systems back to normal operation after the threat has been removed. Carefully restore affected systems into production, making sure everything is clean and stable before reconnecting them. Be sure to test each system, verify that it’s functioning correctly, and monitor it closely for any unusual activity. Your goal is to return to full operation without reopening the door to another attack.
